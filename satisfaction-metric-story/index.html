A smile is not a cure.

Patient satisfaction scores have become the currency of healthcare quality. They measure the wrong thing.

Chapter 1 · The Premise

We started counting smiles.

Patient satisfaction surveys like HCAHPS, Press Ganey, and star ratings drive reimbursement, hospital rankings, and surgeon contracts. The instrument is everywhere. The signal it measures is not what most people assume.

Satisfaction is shaped by hotel-grade experience as much as by clinical outcome.Murasko et al.
Chapter 2 · The Test

Does the score actually track the outcome?

We compared patient satisfaction scores to validated, outcome-anchored measures of orthopaedic care: PROMs that capture pain, function, mobility, and return-to-activity. If satisfaction is a quality metric, the two should travel together. They did not.

Chapter 3 · The Finding

A patient can love their care and still be in pain.

Patients who scored their orthopaedic experience as excellent did not consistently report better functional outcomes. Satisfaction tracked with bedside manner, parking, wait times, room cleanliness, and discharge friendliness, the things hospital administrators can engineer, but not with whether the joint actually worked better afterward.

Chapter 4 · What It Means

Measure what the patient came for.

If satisfaction does not reflect outcome, anchoring reimbursement and ranking to it rewards the wrong work. The argument is not to ignore the patient's experience. It is to stop pretending the experience score is the outcome score. Quality measurement needs to start with what the patient came for, less pain, more function, a life back. Anything else is a proxy.

A satisfied patient is not the same as a well-treated patient.closing line
