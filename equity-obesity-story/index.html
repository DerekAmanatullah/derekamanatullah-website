Chapter 1 · The Policy

A line was drawn at BMI 40.

Across the United States, hospitals and surgeons routinely deny elective joint replacement to patients above a fixed body-mass-index threshold. The justification is risk reduction. The consequence is that the patients with the worst joint pain, the lowest mobility, and the steepest disability curves are sent home — often disproportionately Black, female, or low-income. The cutoff feels objective. It is not.

A policy that excludes the sickest patients in the name of safety must explain what safer means.— Abella et al.
Chapter 2 · The Question

If BMI is the danger, why does some high-BMI surgery go fine?

Using the ACS-NSQIP database, the team analyzed more than 270,000 total hip arthroplasties across the BMI spectrum. They asked a question the BMI-cutoff framework cannot answer: is the risk we attribute to obesity actually about the patient, or about the operation?

Chapter 3 · The Finding

Operative time was doing the work the whole time.

When operative time was held constant, the complication rate for class III obese patients converged toward that of normal-weight patients. The increase in adverse events was not a function of fat. It was a function of how long the case took — and high-BMI cases take longer.

Time
not weight
drives the obesity-associated complication signal in THA.

The implication reverses the policy logic. Faster surgery in high-BMI patients does not eliminate risk — but it eliminates the gap. The patient is not the problem. The operation is.

Chapter 4 · What It Means

Fix the system, not the patient.

If complications scale with operative time, the rational levers are surgical: dedicated teams, refined exposure, simulator-based training for high-BMI anatomy, anesthesia protocols that hold time pressure, and case-volume thresholds at the surgeon and center level. None of those levers require turning a patient away.
