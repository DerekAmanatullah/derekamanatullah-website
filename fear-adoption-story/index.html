Fear is the engine of adoption.

Optimism, curiosity, ambition get the credit. But it is anxiety that moves new technology from the lab into your operating room.

Chapter 1 · Five Fears, One Curve

Every adopter is running on a different anxiety.

The Gartner Hype Cycle and Rogers' Adoption Curve are the two maps every industry uses to predict whether a new technology will live or die. Overlay them and an under-discussed truth emerges. Each adopter group is pushed onto the curve by a specific fear, and industry has learned to sell to each one.

Innovation Trigger
Innovators (+2.5%)
Fear of irrelevance motivates risk-taking with unproven tools.
Peak of Expectations
Early Adopters (+13.5%)
Fear of lost prestige drives bold promises and leadership signaling.
Trough of Disillusionment
Early Majority (+34%)
Fear of failure, loss, embarrassment triggers retreat until evidence emerges.
Slope of Enlightenment
Late Majority (+34%)
Fear of repeating the past demands proof, case studies, peer validation.
Plateau of Productivity
Laggards (+16%)
Fear of obsolescence forces adoption once alternatives vanish.
Fear reshapes itself across phases for each adopter, acting as the constant undercurrent.Blumenfeld et al.
Chapter 2 · Two Bearings, Two Endings

Same hype. Same patients. Different ending.

In the early 2000s, two technologies promised to solve the polyethylene wear problem. Both raced through innovation trigger and peak of expectations. Only one made it across the chasm.

Plateau of Productivity

Highly Crosslinked Polyethylene

  • ✓ Lab data showed dramatic wear reduction
  • ✓ Survived early brittleness concerns
  • ✓ Registries confirmed lower revision rates
  • ✓ Now the worldwide standard of care
Collapsed in the Trough

Metal-on-Metal Bearings

  • ✗ Promised "virtually no wear" for young patients
  • ✗ Pseudotumors, adverse local tissue reactions
  • ✗ Elevated systemic metal ion levels
  • ✗ Most designs withdrawn by the early 2010s
  • HXLPE survived because both relative and absolute gains were durable.
  • MoM collapsed because once registries reported absolute complication rates, the relative-risk story stopped working.
  • The difference wasn't marketing intensity. It was whether long-term, real-world data backed the promise.
Chapter 3 · The Millennial Patient & Five-Star FOMO

When the patient changed, the surgeon's fear changed too.

  • 1970s to 1990s, hip replacement was for older patients with end-stage rheumatoid or severe degenerative disease. The goal was pain relief.
  • 2000s onward: the population shifted to younger, active osteoarthritis patients who wanted to keep playing sports.
  • HXLPE delivered durable performance for this group, and locked higher expectations in as the new baseline.
  • Industry calls this the "millennial patient" and uses it to sell every subsequent technology.

Five-Star FOMO

  • Public physician star ratings are now a reputational and volume driver.
  • Surgeons who recommend against surgery, even within shared decision-making, receive lower ratings, regardless of outcome.
  • So the fear flips: not "did I cause harm," but "did I lose the patient."
Ratings serve as an external emotional amplifier within the adoption curve.from the paper

DAA: A Case Study in Aggregate Harm

  • 50 to 100 cases required to reach posterior-approach complication rates.
  • Industry-sponsored training accelerated uptake faster than the learning curve allowed.
  • Femoral fracture and early loosening rates were borne by patients during the curve.
  • Even after technique stabilized, the cumulative beneficence math is unfavorable.
Chapter 4 · When Academia Becomes Industry

"50% risk reduction" sounds transformative. Two percent to one percent does not.

Relative Risk Reduction
50%
The marketing number. Generates excitement, urgency, fear of missing the breakthrough.
Absolute Risk Reduction
1%
The same data. From a 2% baseline event rate down to 1%. Patients feel this number, not the first one.
  • BMI ≥ 40 kg/m² carries a "two-fold" higher complication rate, an absolute increase often of 1 to 2%.
  • Albumin, low values flagged as strong predictors of complications, heavily confounded by frailty and comorbidities.
  • HbA1c ≥ 8.0%, "doubled" infection risk justifies rigid cutoffs, though the absolute increase is only a few percentage points.
  • Each threshold transformed a modest association into a hard exclusion criterion.
When industry sponsorship aligns with academic publications emphasizing relative benefits, the adoption curve accelerates dramatically.Blumenfeld et al.
  • Industry hype cycle: marketing, training programs, conference sponsorship.
  • Academic hype cycle, publication, citation, and guideline thresholds, all amplifying relative risk.
  • When the two align (e.g., DAA), the combined weight pushes technologies through the curve faster than evidence can correct them.
  • Payor-driven prior authorization adds a third force, sometimes blocking innovation, sometimes locking in premature adoption.
Chapter 5 · Robotics, Training & the Way Out

Robotics is the next DAA, without the early adverse event to slow it.

  • Robotic THA is currently surging along the peak of expectations, driven by industry, peer adoption, and marketing, not yet by robust long-term outcomes.
  • The driver is positional FOMO: institutions fear that failing to adopt means losing innovation leadership.
  • Unlike DAA, robotics has not yet produced a direct, surgeon-attributable adverse event (fracture, nerve palsy) to trigger abandonment.
  • Open question: will future arthroplasty surgeons still know how to do a manual hip?

Moving Forward: Knowledge is Power

  • Privilege absolute risk over relative risk in every counseling and policy decision.
  • Demand peer-reviewed, registry-grade evidence before adoption, not industry-sponsored case series.
  • Treat lab thresholds (BMI, albumin, HbA1c) as risk modifiers, not algorithmic exclusions.
  • Build training pathways that preserve manual technique alongside robotic platforms.
  • Recognize fear when it shows up as irrelevance, prestige, embarrassment, or obsolescence, then ask whether it, or the evidence, is making the call.
Only through awareness of and vigilance against fear can our specialty advance safely and ethically.closing line
